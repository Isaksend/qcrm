\subsection{Backend Framework Comparison}
 
Three Python web frameworks were evaluated for the backend role:
FastAPI, Django with Django REST Framework, and Flask.
\Cref{tab:frameworkcomp} summarises the comparison.
 
\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Backend framework comparison}
  \label{tab:frameworkcomp}
  \begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{}L{3.2cm} L{2.8cm} L{2.8cm} L{2.8cm}@{}}
    \toprule
    \thead{Criterion} & \thead{FastAPI} & \thead{Django + DRF} &
    \thead{Flask} \\
    \midrule
    Native async support & Full (ASGI) & Partial & Limited \\
    Auto OpenAPI schema  & Built-in    & Via drf-spectacular & Via flasgger \\
    ORM                  & SQLAlchemy (external) & Django ORM & SQLAlchemy (external) \\
    Type-safety          & Strong (Pydantic) & Serialisers & Manual \\
    Performance          & Very high   & Moderate    & High \\
    Built-in admin       & No          & Yes         & No \\
    Verdict              & \textbf{Selected} & Runner-up & Not selected \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}
\end{table}

FastAPI was selected for its native ASGI async support (beneficial for
concurrent ML inference requests), automatic OpenAPI schema generation
from Python type annotations, and Pydantic-based request/response
validation. SQLAlchemy's async session interface
(\texttt{AsyncSession} with \texttt{asyncpg}) complements FastAPI's async
model and provides an expressive, database-agnostic ORM layer. The absence
of a built-in admin interface, a notable advantage of Django, is mitigated
by the use of pgAdmin and direct API access during development.

\section{Technology Stack Selection}
 
Technology selection was governed by four criteria: team competency,
documentation quality, open-source licence compatibility, and architectural
fit with the monolithic pattern. \Cref{tab:techstack} presents the selected
stack with rationale.

  \centering

  \label{tab:techstack}

      ASGI-native, full async support, automatic OpenAPI schema
      generation from Python type annotations \\
    ORM & SQLAlchemy &
      Mature, expressive, supports async sessions via \texttt{asyncpg};
      database-agnostic \\
    ML library & scikit-learn + XGBoost + SHAP &
      Mature pipeline API, SHAP TreeExplainer for per-prediction

    Frontend & Vue.js~3 &
      Composition API, progressive framework, large ecosystem \\
    UI components & Vuetify~3 &
      Material Design, comprehensive data-table and chart support \\
    State management & Pinia &
      Lightweight, TypeScript-friendly state store \\
    Database & PostgreSQL &
      ACID compliance, native JSONB support for tags and metadata,
      runs as a Docker container \\
    Background tasks & FastAPI \texttt{BackgroundTasks} &
      In-process async task dispatch for batch ML inference and

      Automated \texttt{pytest} run on PR; SSH-based redeploy on
      merge to \texttt{main} \\
    API documentation & FastAPI built-in (OpenAPI) &
      Automatic schema from type annotations, served at \texttt{/docs} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}
\end{table}
 
\section{High-Level Architecture}

TinyCRM follows a three-tier monolithic architecture: a presentation
tier (Vue.js~3 SPA), an application tier (FastAPI monolith), and a data tier 
(PostgreSQL~15 with Redis~7 cache).
The ML pipeline is embedded within the FastAPI application as a dedicated
router module, with trained models serialised to disk using \texttt{joblib}
and loaded into memory at application start-up. The rationale for embedding
rather than externalising the ML models is consistent with the
``monolith first'' principle \citep{fowler2014microservices}: the extraction
boundary is clearly defined (the \texttt{/ml} router) and can be promoted
to an independent microservice in a future iteration.

\texttt{future url}. Full OpenAPI~3.0 schema is

\section{ML Feature Engineering Specification}
 
\Cref{tab:features} documents the 14~features constructed for both ML
models. Behavioural engagement features (activity counts, email open rate,
days since last contact) dominate in importance, consistent with the
finding of \citet{tsai2010} that interaction history predicts conversion
more reliably than demographic attributes.
 
\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{ML feature engineering specification}
  \label{tab:features}
  \begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{}L{3.2cm} C{1.1cm} L{5.0cm} C{2.3cm}@{}}
    \toprule
    \thead{Feature Name} & \thead{Type} & \thead{Derivation} &
    \thead{Model(s)} \\
    \midrule
    activity\_count\_30d      & Int   & COUNT(activities) in past 30 days        & Lead + Churn \\
    email\_open\_rate         & Float & email\_opens / emails\_sent (90 days)    & Lead \\
    call\_count\_90d          & Int   & COUNT(type=call) in past 90 days         & Lead + Churn \\
    meeting\_count\_90d       & Int   & COUNT(type=meeting) in past 90 days      & Lead \\
    days\_since\_last\_contact & Int  & NOW() minus max(activity.timestamp)      & Lead + Churn \\
    days\_since\_creation     & Int   & NOW() minus contact.lastContact           & Lead + Churn \\
    deal\_value\_open         & Float & SUM(value) for non-closed deals          & Lead \\
    company\_size             & Cat   & contact.company.size (ordinal)           & Lead + Churn \\
    industry                  & Cat   & contact.company.industry (OHE)           & Lead + Churn \\
    deal\_stage\_max          & Cat   & MAX pipeline stage reached (ordinal)     & Lead \\
    tenure\_months            & Int   & Months since first recorded activity      & Churn \\
    monthly\_revenue          & Float & contact.revenue / tenure\_months         & Churn \\
    support\_tickets\_30d     & Int   & Communication logs of type=support, 30d  & Churn \\
    nps\_score                & Float & Last NPS survey response (1--10)         & Churn \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabularx}
\end{table}
