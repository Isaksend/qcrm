\section{Customer Relationship Management}

Customer Relationship Management as a formal discipline emerged from the
intersection of relationship marketing theory and enterprise information
systems in the early 1990s. The most widely cited academic definition is
provided by \citet{payne2005}, who characterise CRM as a strategic approach
concerned with creating improved shareholder value through the development
of appropriate relationships with key customers and customer segments. From
a systems perspective, CRM encompasses three functional dimensions:
operational CRM (the automation of sales, marketing, and service
processes), analytical CRM (the application of data warehousing and mining
to understand customer behaviour), and collaborative CRM (the
multi-channel coordination of customer interactions) \citep{buttle2019}.

\citet{buttle2019} further position CRM software as the technological
infrastructure that operationalises relationship marketing strategy,
cautioning that CRM value derives not from the software platform itself but
from the organisational processes and data discipline that accompany
implementation. This observation is consistent with the persistently high
CRM project failure rates reported in practitioner literature, where
40--70\% of implementations are estimated to fall short of their stated
objectives \citep{gartner2023crm}.

For SMEs specifically, \citet{nguyen2013} identify a distinct set of CRM
adoption success factors, finding that perceived ease of use,
top-management support, and cost-effectiveness are the principal
determinants of adoption in small business contexts, factors that are
systematically underweighted in large-enterprise CRM research. These
findings directly inform TinyCRM's design philosophy, which prioritises
interface simplicity, low configuration overhead, and total cost of
ownership optimisation.

\section{SaaS Architecture Patterns}

Software-as-a-Service delivery has become the dominant CRM deployment
model, with SaaS deployments comprising approximately 87\% of total CRM
implementations as of 2023 \citep{gartner2023crm}. The SaaS model affords
SMEs predictable subscription pricing, the elimination of on-premise
infrastructure costs, automatic updates, and ubiquitous browser-based
access.

From an architectural standpoint, SaaS applications must address
multi-tenancy, data isolation, horizontal scalability, and availability.
\citet{bezemer2010} classify multi-tenancy approaches on a spectrum from
simple hosting (shared nothing, one database per tenant) to true
multi-tenancy (shared schema with a tenant discriminator column). For an
MVP targeting a small initial user base, a single-tenant-per-deployment
model substantially reduces architectural complexity while preserving the
operational benefits of cloud hosting.

\citet{richardson2018} argues that while microservices architectures offer
scalability and team autonomy benefits, the operational complexity they
introduce, including distributed tracing, service mesh management, and
independent deployment pipelines, is disproportionate for early-stage
applications. He explicitly recommends initiating development with a
well-structured monolith and migrating to microservices only upon the
identification of specific scaling bottlenecks. This recommendation is
directly adopted in TinyCRM's architectural decision-making, as described
in \cref{chap:design}.

\citet{fowler2014microservices} articulate the ``monolith first'' pattern:
construct a modular monolith whose internal boundaries correspond to future
microservice candidates, enabling extraction without complete rewrite. This
pattern is particularly appropriate for student development teams, where
the cognitive and operational overhead of distributed systems would likely
overwhelm functional delivery capacity.

\section{Machine Learning for Lead Scoring}

Lead scoring, the prioritisation of sales prospects by estimated conversion
probability, has evolved from rule-based demographic heuristics (the BANT
framework: Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline) to ML-driven approaches
capable of integrating hundreds of behavioural signals. The survey by
\citet{baesens2002} demonstrates that ensemble methods, specifically
Gradient Boosting and Random Forest classifiers, consistently outperform
logistic regression and single decision trees across multiple industry
datasets, a finding subsequently replicated by \citet{chen2016xgboost} in
the context of the XGBoost algorithm.

A critical insight from \citet{verbeke2012} is that raw predictive accuracy
is an insufficient criterion for evaluating business ML models:
interpretability (that is, understanding which features drive a specific
lead score) is essential for sales team adoption and regulatory
accountability. This observation motivates the integration of SHAP
(SHapley Additive exPlanations) values \citep{lundberg2017shap} into
TinyCRM's lead scoring API, providing feature-level explanations alongside
probability outputs.

Regarding feature importance, \citet{tsai2010} demonstrate that
behavioural engagement features (email open rates, call frequency, time
since last contact) have significantly higher predictive power than
demographic features (company size, industry) when both sets are available.
This finding is reflected in TinyCRM's feature engineering specification,
which centres on interaction history derived from the activity log.

\section{Customer Churn Prediction}

Customer churn represents one of the most economically consequential events
in the customer lifecycle. The asymmetry between customer acquisition cost
and retention cost, estimated at a ratio of 5:1 to 7:1 in favour of
retention \citep{reichheld2001}, makes churn prediction a high-return
machine learning application.

The methodological benchmark study by \citet{verbeke2012}, conducted on
telecommunications churn data, remains the standard reference in this
domain. The study demonstrates that Random Forest and Gradient Boosting
consistently achieve AUC-ROC values in the range 0.82--0.89 on standard
benchmarks, outperforming both Logistic Regression and neural networks on
most evaluation metrics. Logistic Regression nevertheless retains practical
value as an interpretable baseline, particularly when feature engineering
is thorough \citep{coussement2008}.

Class imbalance is a critical methodological challenge in churn modelling,
as churn events typically constitute only 5--25\% of the customer
population within any given observation window. Standard mitigation
strategies include SMOTE oversampling \citep{chawla2002}, cost-sensitive
learning via class weighting, and decision threshold calibration.
TinyCRM's training pipeline applies SMOTE exclusively within training
folds during cross-validation to prevent data leakage, and evaluates models
using AUC-ROC and precision-recall area under the curve (PR-AUC) in
preference to raw accuracy.

conducted to identify feature gaps pertinent to the Kazakhstani SME
context. The comparison criteria were drawn from the SME adoption factors
identified by \citet{nguyen2013} and supplemented with data sovereignty and
ML intelligence dimensions specific to this project's context.

The analysis reveals that no existing system simultaneously satisfies all
four Kazakhstani SME requirements: affordable pricing, personal data
localisation within Kazakhstan, native ML-driven predictive intelligence,
and Kazakh-language support. TinyCRM is explicitly positioned to address
this gap.

Bitrix24 and AmoCRM represent the closest competitive alternatives in the
CIS market; however, neither offers ML-driven scoring as a native feature,
and their data residency on European or Russian servers generates
compliance risk under Law~No.~94-V \citep{kazakhstanlaw94v}. SuiteCRM's
open-source self-hosted model provides the greatest data control but
requires DevOps expertise beyond the typical Kazakhstani SME's operational
capacity, and it provides no predictive ML capabilities.

\section{Cloud Infrastructure in Kazakhstan}

The Kazakhstani cloud market has matured considerably since the
commencement of the Digital Kazakhstan programme. The primary domestic
providers relevant to this project are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{QazCloud (qazcloud.kz):} A state-affiliated cloud platform
  operating under the supervision of the Ministry of Digital Development,
  providing IaaS (virtual machines, block and object storage) and PaaS
  (managed Kubernetes, managed PostgreSQL). Data centres are located in
  Almaty and Nur-Sultan and are certified for personal data processing
  under Kazakhstani law.

  \item \textbf{KazHosting (kazhosting.kz):} A commercial VPS and
  dedicated hosting provider with data centres in Almaty, offering
  lower-cost virtual servers from approximately KZT~3,000 per month
  (USD~6.50), suitable for development and small-scale staging workloads.

  \item \textbf{DataLine Kazakhstan:} A subsidiary of the DataLine group
  operating as a Kazakhstani legal entity with a local data centre,
  providing premium colocation and managed cloud services.
\end{itemize}

International providers, including AWS, Google Cloud Platform, and
Microsoft Azure, are accessible from Kazakhstan but route traffic through
non-Kazakhstani regions (typically Frankfurt or Singapore), creating
personal data residency compliance risk. AWS Local Zones had not been
deployed within Kazakhstan as of the time of writing. TinyCRM's
deployment architecture targets QazCloud's Almaty region for production
and KazHosting VPS for development and staging.

\section{Summary and Research Gap}

The literature review establishes five foundational conclusions. First, CRM
is a well-theorised discipline with identifiable SME adoption success
factors centred on simplicity, cost, and perceived usefulness
\citep{nguyen2013}. Second, SaaS monolithic architectures are the
appropriate starting point for MVP-stage systems \citep{richardson2018,
fowler2014microservices}. Third, Gradient Boosting and Random Forest are
the state-of-the-art approaches for lead scoring and churn prediction
respectively \citep{verbeke2012, chen2016xgboost}. Fourth, model
interpretability via SHAP is a necessary complement to raw predictive
performance for business ML applications \citep{lundberg2017shap}. Fifth,
no existing CRM system simultaneously satisfies Kazakhstani SME
requirements for affordability, data residency, ML intelligence, and
localisation. The gap thus identified motivates the TinyCRM development
described in the remainder of this paper.
